# Environmental & Statutory Checks — guide

Reference for sections 5a–5h of `/wa-property-report`. These are the checks outside planning and heritage that architects miss most often — and the ones that cost design time when discovered late.

## 1. Contaminated Sites — DWER

**Source**: [DWER Contaminated Sites Database](https://www.wa.gov.au/service/environment/environment-information-services/contaminated-sites-database)

### Legal framework

- *Contaminated Sites Act 2003* (WA)
- *Contaminated Sites Regulations 2006*
- Administered by **DWER** (Department of Water and Environmental Regulation)

### Classifications

When a site is reported to DWER, it's assessed and given one of these classifications:

| Classification | Meaning | Implications |
|---|---|---|
| **Report not substantiated** | Investigation showed no contamination, no further action | Clean — but Memorial may still be on title for the assessment history |
| **Possibly contaminated — investigation required** | Preliminary evidence suggests contamination; full assessment needed | Phase 1 / Phase 2 ESA required; Memorial on title |
| **Contaminated — remediation required** | Contamination confirmed; must be remediated | Active remediation obligation; likely cost + time impact; Memorial |
| **Contaminated — restricted use** | Contamination present but acceptable for specified use (not others) | Use is constrained; change of use may trigger further investigation |
| **Remediated for restricted use** | Site has been cleaned up to specified use standards | Use restriction runs with the land; Memorial |
| **Not classified** | No report has been made to DWER | Doesn't mean clean — means no record. Check history |

### How to check

1. Go to the Contaminated Sites database on the DWER / WA Government website
2. Search by address — returns any classifications attached to the site
3. Cross-check the Certificate of Title for a **Contaminated Sites Memorial** (under the 2003 Act)
4. Review historical aerial imagery (via NearMap, Google Earth historical) — evidence of prior industrial, fuel station, panel shop, dry cleaner, or orchard use is a flag

### Design implications

If the site has any contamination classification:
- **Phase 1 ESA** (desktop + walkover) — mandatory starting point; $3–8k
- **Phase 2 ESA** (soil + groundwater sampling) — if Phase 1 flags anything; $10–40k+
- **Site Classification Report** by a DWER-accredited **contaminated sites auditor** for classified sites
- **Management plan** if remediation is required
- Design implications:
  - Basement depths — excavation may uncover / disturb contaminated material; capping and removal costs
  - Slab and vapour barrier details — for sites with VOC contamination
  - Landscape / edible gardens — soil replacement zones; restrictions on fruit/vegetable cultivation
  - Groundwater extraction — restrictions if groundwater is contaminated

### Common WA contamination sources

- **Former fuel stations** — hydrocarbons, benzene
- **Panel shops / auto workshops** — solvents, heavy metals, hydrocarbons
- **Dry cleaners** — PCE, TCE (chlorinated solvents)
- **Former orchards / market gardens** — organochlorine pesticides (DDT, dieldrin), arsenic (historical lead-arsenate)
- **Former industrial land** — heavy metals, hydrocarbons, asbestos
- **Landfills and tip sites** — historical tips in Perth are surprisingly common; methane, leachate
- **Fill of unknown origin** — imported fill is often contaminated; common on reclaimed coastal sites

## 2. Bushfire-Prone Areas — DFES

**Source**: [DFES Map of Bush Fire Prone Areas](https://www.dfes.wa.gov.au/regulations/planning-and-development/bushfire-prone-areas)

### Legal framework

- *Planning and Development Act 2005* s. 262
- **SPP 3.7** — Planning in Bushfire Prone Areas
- **AS 3959–2018** — Construction of buildings in bushfire-prone areas
- Designated BPAs reviewed and re-gazetted annually by the **OBRM** (Office of Bushfire Risk Management)

### What it means if the site is in a BPA

- **BAL assessment** is required for any DA involving habitable buildings
- The BAL rating (BAL-LOW, BAL-12.5, BAL-19, BAL-29, BAL-40, BAL-FZ) sets construction requirements under AS 3959
- BAL-40 and BAL-FZ trigger additional **SPP 3.7** planning requirements:
  - **Bushfire Management Plan (BMP)** prepared by a qualified Bushfire Planning Practitioner (BPP)
  - Evacuation considerations
  - Strategic / local-scale bushfire risk treatment
- BAL assessments cost $1.5–5k (BPP engagement)

### Checks on the site

1. Look up the address on the DFES BPA map — Yes / No BPA
2. If BPA, consider the **vegetation type** and **slope** visible on aerial — these drive BAL rating
3. For serious DD (especially rural / hills sites), engage a BPP for preliminary BAL advice

## 3. Coastal — SPP 2.6

**Source**: [DPLH Coastal Planning](https://www.wa.gov.au/organisation/department-of-planning-lands-and-heritage/coastal-planning)

### Legal framework

- **SPP 2.6** — State Coastal Planning Policy
- Applies to land within the coastal zone (usually within 100 m of the coast, but varies)
- LGA may have a declared **CHRMAP** (Coastal Hazard Risk Management and Adaptation Plan) with local-specific setbacks

### What it means

- **Setback from coastal hazard line** for three horizons:
  - **S1 (2030)** — short-term erosion and inundation
  - **S2 (2070)** — medium-term, with sea-level rise allowance
  - **S3 (2110)** — long-term, larger SLR allowance
- Development within the setback is generally not permitted, or requires specific coastal engineering advice
- LGAs with CHRMAPs: Cottesloe, Fremantle (South Beach), Rockingham, Mandurah, Bunbury, Busselton, and others

### Checks

1. Is the site within ~100 m of the coast (ocean or major estuary)?
2. If yes, does the LGA have a CHRMAP?
3. What's the coastal hazard setback at the site?
4. Is the proposed building within the setback?

### Design implications

- Coastal engineers required for feasibility
- Finished floor levels typically set by CHRMAP above defined flood/inundation levels
- Relocatable vs. permanent structures — some councils permit relocatable in the front portion of a coastal lot
- Foundation design for erosion scenarios
- Durability of materials for coastal exposure

## 4. Flood

**Source**: LGA flood maps (usually in LPS as Special Control Areas) + state / catchment flood studies

### Checks

1. Is the site on the LGA's flood map?
2. If yes, 100-year ARI floodway, 100-year extent, or 500-year extent?
3. Finished floor level requirements (typically 500 mm freeboard above 100-year level)
4. Overland flow paths across the site

### Design implications

- Finished floor levels
- Slab-on-ground vs. raised floor
- Habitable rooms above flood level
- Storage / parking on lower levels (generally permitted in floodway at basement / sub-floor levels)

## 5. Aboriginal Heritage — AHIS

**Source**: [Aboriginal Heritage Inquiry System (AHIS)](https://www.wa.gov.au/organisation/department-of-planning-lands-and-heritage/aboriginal-heritage-inquiry-system-ahis)

### Legal framework

- ***Aboriginal Heritage Act 1972*** (WA) — currently in force (as of 2026)
- The 2021 Act was repealed before full commencement; the 1972 Act remains the operative law
- The Act makes it an offence to damage, alter, or excavate an **Aboriginal site** without consent

### Types of heritage listings

| Status | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Registered Site** | Formally registered Aboriginal site — Section 5 protections apply |
| **Other Heritage Place** | Recorded but not formally registered — less statutory force, still significant |
| **Lodged** | New sites / reports recently lodged, pending assessment |
| **Stored Data / Restricted** | Information withheld from public disclosure |

### What it means

- If a Registered Site is on or near your site, any works that might disturb it require **Section 18 consent** from the Minister for Aboriginal Affairs
- Section 18 consent involves consultation with the relevant Traditional Owner group(s)
- Can take 6–18 months and is not guaranteed
- **Heritage Agreements** with Native Title holders or Aboriginal corporations may also be required
- Failing to check and acting in breach is a serious offence

### Checks

1. Search AHIS by address — returns registered sites and other heritage places within / near the site
2. Note the site type (burial, artefact scatter, ceremonial, camp, etc.)
3. Identify the **Traditional Owner / Native Title party** for the area (Whadjuk Noongar for most of Perth metro; the **Noongar Boodja / SWALSC** agreement covers the south-west)
4. **Always** recommend legal and cultural advice for any site with AHIS findings

### Note

The *Aboriginal Heritage Act 1972* has been politically contested. In 2021, a new Act was passed but repealed in 2023 before full commencement. As at 2026 the 1972 Act applies. **Check current legal framework** at time of DD — this has been a moving area.

## 6. Flora, Fauna, TECs

**Source**: [NatureMap (DBCA)](https://naturemap.dbca.wa.gov.au/)

### Legal framework

- *Biodiversity Conservation Act 2016* (WA) — threatened species, TECs, ecological communities
- *Environmental Protection Act 1986* (WA) — clearing of native vegetation
- *Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999* (Cth) — matters of national environmental significance (MNES)

### What to check

- **Threatened Ecological Communities (TECs)** — mapped in NatureMap
- **Priority Ecological Communities (PECs)** — lower threshold, still protected in some contexts
- **Threatened / priority flora** — e.g. declared rare flora
- **Threatened fauna** — e.g. black cockatoo habitat
- **Banksia woodland (TEC)** — common on Perth's Swan Coastal Plain; significant constraint
- **Significant trees** — LGA tree registers, often containing individual trees of local importance

### Design implications

- Environmental consultant required if any findings
- Native vegetation clearing permit under EPA if > 1 ha of native vegetation to be cleared
- EPBC referral if MNES is triggered (black cockatoo, specific TECs)
- Tree retention strategies — integrate with landscape design early

## 7. Acid Sulfate Soils (ASS)

**Source**: [DWER acid sulfate soil risk mapping](https://www.wa.gov.au/service/environment/environment-information-services/acid-sulfate-soils-information)

### What they are

Acid sulfate soils contain iron sulfide compounds formed in marine / estuarine sediments. When exposed to air (e.g. by excavation, dewatering), they oxidise and produce sulfuric acid — damaging to concrete, steel, groundwater, and vegetation.

### Where they are in WA

- Low-lying coastal and estuarine areas (< 5 m AHD)
- Swan Coastal Plain wetlands and lakes
- South-west coastal fringes
- Pilbara and Kimberley coastal flats

### Checks

1. Site elevation (< 5 m AHD?)
2. DWER ASS risk maps — Low / Moderate / High risk
3. If High: preliminary soils investigation required before design

### Design implications

- Basement and deep excavation design — containment, treatment, disposal
- Dewatering — plans must be approved
- Fill and capping strategies
- Chemical selection for concrete (sulfate-resistant cement)

## 8. Services & In-Ground

Not strictly a DD environmental check but worth scanning:

- **Overhead powerlines / transformer kiosks / substations** — clearances; may need Western Power relocation; expensive
- **Sewer** — mains location and depth; set by Water Corp; may constrain basement
- **Gas** — ATCO assets
- **Telecommunications** — NBN / Telstra pits and cables
- **Main Roads WA** — if adjoining a state road, access controls apply

Dial Before You Dig (1100.com.au) provides free service location reports — typically done at construction phase, but worth doing at DD if basement or complex services are proposed.
