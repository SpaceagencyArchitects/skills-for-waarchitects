# Heritage Check — guide

Reference for the heritage section of `/wa-property-report`. Heritage is layered in WA — State, local, and area-based — and the implications for a DA differ at each level.

## The three layers of WA heritage

### 1. State Register of Heritage Places

- Administered by the **Heritage Council of WA** under the ***Heritage Act 2018* (WA)**
- Lists places of State significance
- Registered places must be maintained; substantial works require **Heritage Council referral and approval**
- Approx. 1,500 places currently on the Register
- Search at [inHerit](https://inherit.dplh.wa.gov.au/)

**Design implications:**
- Works must be consistent with the *Burra Charter* (Australia ICOMOS)
- A **Heritage Impact Statement (HIS)** by a qualified heritage consultant is required for any external works
- A **Conservation Plan / Conservation Management Plan** may be required for major works
- Heritage Council referral is a statutory requirement — add 4–8 weeks to the DA timeline
- Demolition or substantial alteration requires specific justification; "because we want to" is not enough
- Some grants / tax concessions may be available via the Heritage Council

### 2. Local Heritage Survey (LHS) / Municipal Inventory (MI)

- Administered by each **Local Government Authority**
- LHS is the current name (post-2018); older documents may be called "Municipal Inventory"
- Lists places of local heritage significance
- Controls vary by **management category** (usually A / B / C or 1 / 2 / 3)

**Typical management categories:**

| Category | Significance | Typical controls |
|---|---|---|
| **A** or **1** | Highest level of local significance — essential to conserve | Substantial controls; demolition unlikely to be approved; significant alterations require HIS; treated similarly to State Register at LGA level |
| **B** or **2** | High local significance — worthy of conservation | HIS required for substantial works; fabric conservation expected; sympathetic alterations permitted |
| **C** or **3** | Moderate local significance — conservation desirable | Design review required; alterations must respect character; demolition possible with justification |
| **Exempt** or **Adopted by LHS** | On the register but not subject to LPS controls | Limited statutory weight, but still worth noting for client and design |

**Each LGA's categories differ** — always check the specific LGA's LHS for the exact terminology and controls. Most are now on inHerit.

**Design implications:**
- HIS usually required (category A, B, sometimes C)
- LGA heritage officer referral within the DA assessment process
- If category A or B, expect LGA Design Review Panel referral
- Demolition applications for any listed place attract advertising and likely Council-level determination

### 3. Heritage Areas

- Designated areas within the LPS (under the *Planning and Development Act 2005* and the relevant LPS)
- Apply to a **precinct**, not an individual place
- Typical examples:
  - **Fremantle West End Heritage Area** (City of Fremantle LPS4)
  - **Peppermint Grove Heritage Precinct** (Shire of Peppermint Grove)
  - **Guildford Heritage Precinct** (City of Swan)
  - **Subiaco Heritage Area** (City of Subiaco)
  - **North Fremantle Heritage Area** (City of Fremantle LPS4)
- Each Heritage Area has a specific **Local Planning Policy (LPP)** setting design controls for new work, alterations, infill, and demolition
- Applies to **every site in the area**, not just individually listed places

**Design implications:**
- LGA Heritage Area LPP is the controlling document — retrieve and read it
- Typical controls:
  - Streetwall heights to match existing
  - Materiality palette (e.g. traditional materials: face brick, render, iron roofing, verandahs)
  - Fenestration patterns
  - Roof forms (e.g. no flat roofs, no modernist forms addressing the street)
  - Fencing types
  - Signage
  - Limited infill — no new development fronting the street if it would break character
- **Contemporary design** is usually still permitted but must demonstrate sympathy with context — not mimicry, not pastiche, but respectful dialogue

## Search sequence for a site

1. **[inHerit](https://inherit.dplh.wa.gov.au/)** — search by address
   - Returns both State Registered places and LHS entries that have been uploaded (most metro LGAs are comprehensive; some regional LGAs less so)
2. **LGA website — Local Heritage Survey** — if inHerit coverage is incomplete, check the LGA's own LHS PDF
3. **LPS zoning map** — check for Heritage Area overlays or Special Control Areas
4. **LPP list on LGA website** — check for any Heritage Area LPP or conservation LPP that applies
5. **Adjoining / nearby sites** — even if your site isn't listed, heritage listings next door or across the street affect design (view corridors, streetscape continuity, overshadowing heritage places)

## Statements of significance

Every registered place has a **statement of significance** — a formal summary of *why* it is significant (historic, aesthetic, social, scientific, spiritual). Read it carefully:

- It identifies the **values to be protected** — fabric, form, relationship to setting, intangible meaning
- It guides which elements are **significant / contributory / neutral / intrusive**
- An HIS must address each significant value and demonstrate how the proposal respects it

## What the skill captures

In the `/wa-property-report` output, the heritage section should record:

1. **State Register** — name, register ref, category, level of significance, statement of significance (summary)
2. **Local Heritage Survey** — name, category, statement (if available)
3. **Heritage Area** — which area, which LPP applies
4. **Adjoining heritage** — notable nearby listings that may affect design
5. **Design implications** — HIS required? Heritage Council referral? Design review?
6. **Approval path** — if heritage-listed, note the additional referral / advertising / timeline implications

For each finding, a clear statement of **what this means for design** — that's what the client / design team needs, not just the register reference.

## Heritage consultants

If any heritage listing applies, recommend engaging a qualified heritage consultant early. They:
- Prepare the HIS / Conservation Plan
- Liaise with Heritage Council of WA (if State-listed)
- Negotiate with LGA heritage officer
- Provide fabric advice on historic buildings (materiality, construction techniques, weathering, compatibility)
- Provide cultural / historical context for new work

WA heritage consultants are typically accredited through the **Australia ICOMOS** professional network.
